28th June 1969

Stonewall

A haven for the most marginalized people in the gay community like drag queens, transgender people, male prostitutes, and homeless youth, the Stonewall Inn was the location for one of the most important moments in LGBT history. It was the only public place where gay men could dance with each other.

While police raids were common in gay bars, owners were often tipped off before the raids would happen. On this particular night, Stonewall employees were not warned.

Creedence Clearwater Revival's Proud Mary was a popular song at the end of 1969. Later to be covered by Tina Turner, this song became a staple in the gay community. The song continues to be an anthem of pride, as evidenced by this gay man at a wedding who steals the show.

Martha P. Johnson and Sylvia Rivera were pivotal players in the riots. Often seen as a white and male moment, the Stonewall Riots were largely instigated by these two trans women of color. Johnson moved to the West Village to escape homophobia back home. On the night of the Stonewall Riots, she was celebrating her 25th birthday. Rivera, 17, went on to be an outspoken critic of racism, sexual violence, and transphobia.

28th June 1970

The First Pride Parade

A year after Stonewall, organizers come together to march from Christopher Street to Central Park. Initially called Christopher Street Liberation Day, the march becomes a template for people all around the world to demand for legal and social recognition.

2nd April 1974

Kathy Kozachenko

The first openly gay or lesbian person to run successfully for political office, Kathy Kozachenko was an outspoken student at the University of Michigan and ralled fellow student radicals to fight for progressive causes. Often overlooked for Harvey Milk, Kozachenko is the actual first out candidate to run and win political office.

In 1974, John Waters released Female Trouble, a dark comedy starring Divine, David Lochary, Mary Vivian Pearce, Mink Stole, Edith Massey, Michael Potter, Cookie Mueller, and Susan Walsh. This scene is later re-created, musically, on RuPaul's Drag Race Season 7.

12th May 1975

Consenting Adult Sex Bill

After five years of debate in the legislature, the Consenting Adult Sex Bill was signed into law in California by Governor Jerry Brown. Assemblyman Willie Brown introduced the bill in conjunction with George Moscone, state senator and later mayor of San Francisco.

In 1975, Elton John released Philadephia Freedom, a song dedicated to tennis legend Billie Jean King. Elton John was a huge fan of King, and wrote the song with Bernie Taupin to express his admiration for a woman who convinced the US Open to award women championships the same amount of money as men.

8th November 1977

Harvey Milk

Harvey Milk is elected city-country supervisor in San Francisco. Milk moved to the bay from NYC during a migration of gay men who settled in the Castro District. Buttressed by the increasingly powerful political constituency of gay men, Milk wins the election.

Star Wars was just released around this time, starring Carrie Fisher. Fisher became an ally to the gay community even after her husband came out and left her. But it wasn't Star Wars that Fisher said connected her to her gay fans; she instead cited her roles in Under the Rainbow and Sorority Row

11th May 1982

The AIDS Crisis

Gay men in the US and Sweden, as well as heterosexual people in Tanzania and Haiti, start showing signs of what would later become known as AIDS in 1978. Called GRID for the first time in a 1982 New York Times article, it was also referred to as "gay cancer" and "gay plague".

Later in 1982, the Weather Girls release It's Raining Men. Later to be re-recorded with RuPaul, the song becomes a gay anthem for well, obvious reasons. For the LGBT community, who largely still relied on discos to come together in puiblic, music became a strong unifying force. The Weather Girls, no stranger to the LGBT community with their work with Sylvester.

March 1987

ACT-UP

Due to a frustratingly slow response by the US Government in the face of AIDS, the AIDS Coalition to Unlease Power is formed. They organize a protest and 17 people are arrested.

Protests continue with the 1987 March on Washington for Lesbian and Gay Rights, which includes the first nationwide gathering of bisexuals, in October. Spurred by both Ronald Reagan's lack of acknowledge of the AIDS crisis and the Supreme Court case of Bowers v Hardwick, the march had seven platform points, including an end to racism in the United States and to apartheid in South Africa.

National Coming Out Day was established a year later in commemoration of the march.

17th May 1990

World Health Organization

The General Assembly of the World Health Organization removed homosexuality from their list of mental disorders. At this point in time, several countries had already decriminalized homosexuality, including Switzerland, Sweden, Suriname, Greece, Jordan, Thailand, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Israel, among others. The United States would not fully do so until 2003.

Madonna's Vogue was released in 1990. While many people think of voguing as being originated by Madonna, voguing actually came from drag balls in queer Black and Latino communities. That same year, Paris Is Burning documents New York City's ballroom scene.

5th November 1991

Sherry Harris

Sherry Harris becomes the first openly lesbian Black person to be elected to office. Once an engineer for Boeing, Harris found the City Council of Seattle to be much more difficult, as various groups wanted her to fight for their causes and their causes only.

In 1991, George Michael and Elton John re-released Don't Let the Sun Go Down On Me. Topping the US and UK Billboard charts, the song generated proceeds that were donated to 10 different charities for children, AIDS, and education.

3rd November 1992

Althea Garrison

Although not out at the time of her election, Althea Garrison became the first transgender person to be elected to public office, to the Boston City Council.

Earlier in the year, the Lesbian Avengers is founded. One of their most enduring legacies is the annual Dyke March. The Lesbian Avengers came to be known for their unique style of protest, often featuring over the top visuals like fire eating.

17th May 1993

Lea Delaria

1993 proves to a big year in pop culture for LGBT people. Lea Delaria is the first openly gay comic to perform in late-night with her appearance on the Arsenio Hall Show.

The same year, RuPaul releases Supermodel (You Better Work) and to her surprise, the song catapults her into mainstream popularity. The song explores themes of confidence and self-esteem. Supermodels continued to be a common theme present in LGBT pop culture.

Although being LGBT was already banned in the army, DADT was passed in 1993 to prevent the armed forces from asking people about their sexual orientation. Originally intended to silence conversations about sexual orientation, DADT came to be seen as an infringement of LGBT equal rights. After all, when you tell LGBT people to be quiet, the opposite often happens.

28th November 1998

Murder of Rita Hester

Many people know about the murder of Matthew Shepard on October 6, 1998. Many don't know about Rita Hester, a transgender Black woman who was murdered in Allston, Massachusetts. Her death would spark the creation of the Transgender Day of Remembrance, observed annually on November 20.

While treatment of HIV made a leap forward with a triple cocktail of drugs, the virus continued to spread and claim lives. The 90s, characterized by a desire for unity and life in the face of an ongoing health crisis and unequal rights, saw the creation of songs like Janet Jackson's Together Again, inspired by Janet's experience of losing a friend to AIDS.

Also in 1998, Tammy Baldwin became the first openly LGBT person to be elected to Congress, winning Wisconsin's 2nd congressional district seat.

Bonus: Dana International from Israel became the first transgender person to win the Eurovision Song Contest with her song Diva.

1st April 2001

Netherlands

The Netherlands was the first country to explicitly pass a law legalizing marriage between two people of the same sex.

A few months later, Hedwig and the Angry Inch was released. Chronicling the story of an East German glam rocker who undergoes a botched sex reassignment operation, the film was later adapted into a musical starring Neil Patrick Harris.

Various 2000s

The Movement Continues

The 2000s sees incredibly rapid progress in LGBT rights, with sodomy laws and anti-same-sex marriage laws being struck down around the world while the United States sees fragmentation as states differ on their attitudes and laws about same-sex marriage. Some countries continue to clamp down on LGBT rights, including Uganda and Honduras, both of which ban same-sex marriage in 2005.

While there is a diversity of attitudes about same-sex marriage even within the LGBT community, same-sex marriage became a crude litmus test of LGBT rights in the United States. The 2000s saw steady move towards the majority of Americans approving of same-sex marriage.

It was in this political climate that RuPaul unleashed RuPaul's Drag Race onto the world in 2009. The show has reached a level of popularity that RuPaul herself couldn't have predicted, now airing on VH1. The show is not without its critics, but the changing tide in the attitudes towards LGBT people and culture allowed the show to proliferate.

YouTuber Tyler Oakley is one of countless voices now using social media to circumvent what was once traditional media channels. Pop culture continues to proliferate largely through individual contributers, generating memes, videos, and ideas directly with each other. Some could argue that without the power of social media, we would not have seen the latest evolution in pride...

June 2017

Inclusivity in the LGBT Community

As we've seen, the LGBT community isn't some monolithic group. Pride often has different meanings depending on their personal experiences. Most recently, there have been several proponents and critics of using a new pride symbol: a rainbow flag with black and brown stripes to signify the importance of people of color within the LGBT community.

A personal note: We've seen so much progress over the last few decades and it's easy to forget the struggles that our communities have faced and overcome to give us the privileges that we enjoy today. But it also seems that while things change, we're still faced with the same internal challenges that we faced so many years ago. For me, pride isn't just about proud of being gay; it's a way for me to be thankful to those who came before while having the hope to overcome the challenges that we'll face in the future. Thanks for being a part of it.
